# Hornbill Aviation — Brand Identity & Writing Style

A dedicated guide for how Hornbill Aviation sounds, writes, and talks to students.

---

## 1. Brand identity

### Brand name

**Hornbill Aviation**

Use the full name on first mention in body copy. After that, "Hornbill" or "the school" is fine. Avoid "HFC" in customer-facing copy unless space is severely constrained.

### Brand story

Most flight schools make you fit their program. Hornbill Aviation builds the program around you.

We're a Part 61 school at Reno–Tahoe (RNO) with a simple idea: the best pilots come from training that respects their schedule, their budget, and their goals. Our fleet of similarly equipped PA28s keeps costs predictable and learning consistent. Students choose their instructor — or bring their own. And when they're ready, they rent the same aircraft for real cross-country trips instead of just repeating the same practice-area maneuvers.

The hornbill in our name is deliberate. Hornbills are distinctive, determined, and built for long flights. That is the kind of pilot we train.

### Brand promise

**We make learning to fly fit your life, not the other way around.**

### Positioning statement

For people in Northern Nevada and beyond who want to learn to fly, Hornbill Aviation is the flexible, student-first Part 61 school that combines consistent aircraft, instructor choice, real cross-country experience, and the most competitive rental rates at RNO.

### What we are not

- Not a fast-track degree mill.
- Not a rigid, one-size-fits-all academy.
- Not an "aviation lifestyle" brand that sells a fantasy more than a syllabus.

## 3. Voice

### Voice attributes

| Attribute | In practice | Sounds like |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Friendly** | Direct address, plain words, human sentences. | "You choose your instructor." |
| **Confident** | State what is true without hedging. | "We maintain every aircraft before each flight." |
| **Inspirational** | Connect the goal to a concrete next step. | "This is the year you fly to Tahoe." |
| **Reassuring** | Name the worry, then answer it. | "New to aviation? Your first lesson is a discovery flight — no commitment." |
| **Encouraging** | Make progress feel normal and achievable. | "Every hour in the logbook gets you closer." |
| **Professional** | Precise, safe, no hype. | "Part 61 training at RNO with CFIs you select." |

### Voice isn't

| Not this | Because |
|----------|---------|
| Cold or corporate | We use "you" and short sentences. |
| Overly casual | We're a flight school, not a buddy's group chat. Safety and credibility matter. |
| Hype-driven | No exclamation points, no fake urgency, no miracle promises. |
| Academic | We don't lecture. We explain what the student needs to know to take the next step. |

## 4. Tone by context

### Homepage

- Warm, confident, slightly ambitious.
- Lead with the student's outcome, then the proof.
- Example: *"Learn to fly on a schedule that fits your life — with instructors you choose, aircraft you can count on, and real cross-country experience."*

### Training programs / certifications

- Clear, structured, outcome-focused.
- Say what each program includes, how long it typically takes, and what it qualifies the student to do.
- Example: *"Private Pilot: 40+ hours of flight training, ground instruction, and solo cross-country experience. Pass the FAA checkride and fly passengers anywhere in the U.S."*

### Pricing / fleet

- Transparent, calm, specific.
- List rates, what's included, and what isn't.
- Example: *"PA28-161: $149/hour wet. No fuel surcharge. No membership fee. Book by the hour."*

### Instructor profiles

- Warm, credible, personal.
- Lead with the human, then the credentials.
- Example: *"Sarah has 2,000 hours instructing in PA28s and specializes in helping anxious students build confidence."*

### Cross-country / real-world flying

- Energetic, grounded, experiential.
- Show what a trip actually looks like.
- Example: *"Plan a route, file a flight plan, and fly to Lake Tahoe, Monterey, or Bend. You handle the decisions; we back you up."*

### Safety / maintenance

- Confident, factual, calm.
- No reassurance theater. Say what you do.
- Example: *"Every aircraft is inspected before each flight and maintained on a recurring schedule. If something doesn't look right, it doesn't fly."*

### Contact / booking

- Direct, low-friction, encouraging.
- Example: *"Questions? Call, text, or book a discovery flight online. Your first lesson is the easiest way to decide if flying is for you."*

## 5. Writing rules

### Do

1. **Write to one reader.** Picture a specific person: a 32-year-old nurse considering a career change, a retiree who always wanted to fly, a college student building hours for the airlines.
2. **Lead with outcomes.** Start with what the student gets, not what the school offers.
3. **Use specific numbers and names.** "PA28-161," "$149/hour wet," "RNO," "Part 61," "40+ hours."
4. **Keep sentences short.** One idea per sentence. Aim for an average under 20 words.
5. **Use active voice.** "You fly the route. We debrief after."
6. **Prefer "you."** Second person keeps the copy student-centered.
7. **Answer objections directly.** Cost, time, safety, flexibility — name them and solve them.
8. **Use aviation terms naturally.** Don't over-explain terms pilots use, but define anything a first-timer won't know.
9. **Make CTAs concrete.** "Book a discovery flight," "See the fleet and rates," "Meet the instructors."
10. **Read it out loud.** If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it.

### Don't

1. **Don't use empty superlatives.** "Best," "premier," "world-class," "top-rated." Prove it instead.
2. **Don't use aviation clichés.** "Take your dreams to new heights," "soar above the rest," "passion for aviation."
3. **Don't force balance.** Avoid sentences like "We offer both flexibility and structure." Own the main thing.
4. **Don't over-explain.** One concrete detail beats a paragraph of justification.
5. **Don't use symmetrical fluff.** "Whether you're a beginner or experienced, hobbyist or professional, young or old..."
6. **Don't use LLM hedges.** "It's important to note," "In today's world," "At the end of the day," "Ultimately."
7. **Don't bury the price.** If you mention affordability, say the number or the range.
8. **Don't write in passive institutional voice.** "Students are provided with..." → "You get..."
9. **Don't use fake urgency.** "Limited spots!" unless genuinely limited and quantified.
10. **Don't sound like a chatbot.** Warm is fine; overly familiar or generic is not.

## 6. Before / after examples

### Example 1: Homepage hero

**Before (don't write this):**
> "At Hornbill Aviation, we are passionate about aviation and committed to providing world-class flight training solutions for aspiring pilots of all backgrounds. Whether you're looking to pursue a professional career or simply want to experience the joy of flight, our team is here to help you unlock your potential and achieve your dreams."

**After (write this):**
> "Learn to fly on your schedule, with instructors you choose and aircraft you can count on. From first lesson to checkride — and your first real cross-country — we train pilots, not passengers."

### Example 2: Pricing

**Before:**
> "We offer competitive and transparent pricing designed to fit your budget, with a variety of aircraft available at rates that make flight training accessible."

**After:**
> "PA28-161: $149/hour wet. No fuel surcharge. No membership fee. The lowest wet rate for a comparable aircraft at RNO."

### Example 3: Cross-country rentals

**Before:**
> "Our rental program gives students the opportunity to gain valuable real-world experience by flying longer cross-country trips, which helps build confidence and decision-making skills in a way that local practice flights cannot."

**After:**
> "Don't just practice maneuvers. Plan a trip, file a route, and go. Students rent the same PA28s they train in and fly to Tahoe, Monterey, or wherever the lesson makes sense."

### Example 4: Instructor choice

**Before:**
> "We have a diverse team of experienced and professional flight instructors who are dedicated to providing personalized training tailored to each student's individual needs and learning style."

**After:**
> "Pick the instructor who fits your schedule and your style. Or bring your own. The relationship matters — and at Hornbill, you get to choose it."

### Example 5: Discovery flight

**Before:**
> "A discovery flight is the perfect way to experience the thrill of aviation for the first time and see if flight training is right for you."

**After:**
> "Your first lesson is a discovery flight. You sit in the left seat, handle the controls, and decide if flying is for you. No commitment."

## 8. Grammar and style conventions

### Capitalization

- Use sentence case for headings: "Start your flight training."
- Capitalize "FAA," "CFI," "Part 61," "PA28," "RNO," "Hornbill Aviation."
- Don't capitalize generic job titles: "flight instructor," "certified flight instructor" unless used as a name.

### Numbers

- Spell out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use numerals for prices, aircraft identifiers, hours, rates, and years: "$149/hour," "PA28-161," "40+ hours," "2026."
- Use "$149/hour wet" not "$149/hour, wet."

### Punctuation

- Use serial commas.
- Keep exclamation points rare. One per page at most, and only for genuine warmth or excitement.
- Avoid em-dashes unless they improve clarity.

### Contractions

- Use them. "We're," "you'll," "don't," "it's" keep the voice human.

### Lists

- Keep list items parallel in structure.
- Start each item with the same part of speech.
- Example:
  - Choose your instructor
  - Train in the same PA28 fleet
  - Fly real cross-country routes

### Links and CTAs

- Link text should describe the destination: "See the fleet and rates" not "Click here."
- CTA buttons should begin with a verb: "Book," "See," "Meet," "Start," "Call."

## 9. Page-level copy guidance

### Homepage

- **Hero headline:** Lead with flexibility and outcome. Keep under 12 words.
- **Subheadline:** Add the concrete proof: instructor choice, PA28 fleet, cross-country trips.
- **Proof strip:** 3–4 stat or benefit lines with real numbers where possible.
- **CTA:** "Book a discovery flight."

### Training programs

- One section per certificate/rating.
- Include: what it is, typical hours, what you can do after, and next step.
- Use real names: Private Pilot, Instrument Rating, Commercial Pilot, CFI.

### Fleet and pricing

- Lead with the transparency promise.
- Table format: aircraft, rate, notes.
- Add a short FAQ: "What's included in 'wet'?" "Can I rent for cross-country?"

### Instructors

- One card per instructor: photo, name, focus area, hours/experience.
- Lead with the human, not the resume.

### Cross-country / real-world flying

- Tell one trip story as an example.
- Include route, aircraft, what the student did, what they learned.
- CTA: "Ask about cross-country rental."

### About

- Origin story of the name and the school.
- Why Part 61, why RNO, why a uniform fleet.
- Keep it brief and specific.

### Contact / booking

- Phone, email, location, booking link.
- Lower the barrier: "No commitment. Just questions or a first flight."

## 10. Review checklist

Before any copy goes live, check:

- [ ] Does it sound like something a real instructor would say to a prospective student?
- [ ] Is the student the subject of the sentence more often than the school?
- [ ] Are all claims specific? (Names, numbers, places, aircraft types.)
- [ ] Are there any clichés, superlatives, or LLM hedges?
- [ ] Is the CTA concrete and action-oriented?
- [ ] Does it answer the obvious worry a reader would have?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the page context?
- [ ] Would you feel confident signing up based on this copy alone?
