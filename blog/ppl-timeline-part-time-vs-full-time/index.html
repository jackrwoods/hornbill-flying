How long does PPL training take part-time vs full-time?

Cost & PlanningAugust 18, 20264 min read

Quick answer

Full-time Private Pilot students at RNO often finish in 3–4 months. Part-time students flying once or twice a week typically take 6–12 months. The difference is not intelligence; it is frequency and consistency.

One of the first questions prospective students ask is how long it takes to earn a Private Pilot certificate. The honest answer depends on how often you can fly, how well you study, and how flexible your schedule is. This guide compares the two most common paths at Hornbill Flight Center.

What the FAA requires

The FAA requires at least 40 hours of flight experience for the Private Pilot certificate, including specific cross-country, night, and solo hours. Most students need more than the minimum. A realistic total at RNO is 55–75 hours before the checkride.

Time to finish depends on how quickly you accumulate those hours and how well you retain what you learn between lessons. That is why frequency matters more than raw talent.

Full-time training: 3–4 months

A full-time student treats flight training like a job. They fly four or five days a week, study ground material daily, and take the written exam within the first month. With this rhythm, skills build on top of each other instead of fading between lessons.

Full-time students often finish in 3–4 months with 55–65 hours. The pace is intense but efficient. It works well for people with flexible schedules, summer breaks, or career-track goals. The downside is cost concentration: you are paying for a lot of flying in a short window.

Part-time training: 6–12 months

Most adult students at Hornbill fly part-time. They have jobs, families, and other responsibilities. A common schedule is two lessons per week: one weekday evening and one weekend morning.

At two lessons per week, a part-time student usually finishes in 6–9 months with 60–75 hours. At one lesson per week, the timeline stretches toward 12 months or longer because each lesson includes review before new material. The total hours often drift higher as well.

The key to part-time success is protecting your lesson slots. Treat them like meetings you cannot move. Cancel only for weather or genuine emergencies.

Ground school and the written exam

Flight training and ground school can run side by side. Students who pass the FAA written exam early have a head start. They already understand regulations, weather, navigation, and aerodynamics, so their CFI can spend airtime teaching practical skills, not explaining theory.

Online ground school, evening classes, or one-on-one sessions with your CFI all work. Pick the format that matches how you learn. The important thing is to start early and finish the written before the checkride looms.

How weather affects the timeline

Reno weather is generally good for training, but it is not perfect. Summer afternoons can be bumpy and hot, making density altitude a real factor. Winter storms can cancel lessons for days. Spring winds can keep students on the ground. A flexible schedule helps you move lessons to better weather windows.

Cost and timeline together

The longer the timeline, the more the total cost can grow, even if the hourly rate stays the same. Part-time students need more review lessons and may repeat skills more often. That is why our cost breakdown for a Private Pilot license in Nevada emphasizes frequency.

You can lower the hourly cost by joining the membership program. Members pay $159/hour wet for the PA28 versus $185/hour for non-members. Over 60 hours, that difference adds up.

Tips for staying on track

  • Book a recurring schedule. Same days, same times, every week.
  • Study between lessons. Even 20 minutes of chair flying helps.
  • Use a syllabus. Know the next two lessons so you can prepare.
  • Take the written early. Do not let it become a last-minute hurdle.
  • Fly often enough to retain skills. Twice a week is the practical minimum for efficient progress.

Which path is right for you?

Choose full-time if you can dedicate several months and want to finish quickly. Choose part-time if you need to balance training with the rest of your life. Both paths lead to the same certificate and the same checkride.

The best first step is to book a discovery flight and talk through your schedule with a CFI. You can also explore the full Private Pilot program and read our complete guide to flight training in Reno.
