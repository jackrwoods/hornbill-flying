If you are researching flight schools in Reno, you have probably seen the terms Part 61 and Part 141. They describe the set of FAA regulations a school follows. The certificate you earn is the same. The checkride is the same. The difference is how you get there.

## What is Part 61?

Part 61 refers to 14 CFR Part 61, the FAA regulations that govern the certification of pilots and flight instructors. Under Part 61, training is typically one-on-one between a student and a CFI. There is no required syllabus, no stage checks, and no formal enrollment calendar. Your instructor signs you off when you meet the aeronautical experience and proficiency requirements for the certificate or rating.

This structure is ideal for working adults, parents, and anyone whose schedule changes from week to week. You book lessons around your life. If you need extra time on landings, you take extra time on landings. If you want to fly twice this week and not at all next week, you can.

Hornbill Flight Center is a Part 61 school because flexibility is one of our core beliefs. We explain why in our [guide to flight training in Reno](/blog/flight-training-reno-nv/).

## What is Part 141?

Part 141 schools operate under an FAA-approved training curriculum. They must meet specific classroom, simulator, and flight-hour requirements, maintain student records in a prescribed format, and pass periodic FAA inspections. Training is usually structured in stages or semesters, with stage checks along the way.

Part 141 makes sense for full-time students who want a college-like structure, for veterans using certain GI Bill benefits, and for career-track pilots who want an accelerated, airline-oriented pathway. It can also allow slightly reduced minimum flight hours for some certificates if the student performs well.

## How the schedules differ

Under Part 61, your schedule is yours. You and your CFI agree on lesson times. You can fly mornings, evenings, or weekends. You can pause training for a month and pick up where you left off without falling behind a class.

Under Part 141, your schedule is largely set by the school. Missing a stage check or falling behind the cohort can delay your completion. That rigidity helps some students stay on track and hurts others who have unpredictable lives.

## How costs differ

Part 141 programs often quote an all-inclusive price up front. That can be reassuring, but it also means you pay for the structure whether you need it or not. Part 61 costs depend on how many hours you need, which means the total is less predictable but often lower for focused students.

At Hornbill, we publish our [PA28 wet rates](/fleet/) openly. Members pay $159/hour; non-members pay $180/hour. There are no hidden fuel surcharges. That transparency lets you control your own budget.

## Which path should you choose?

Choose Part 61 if you:

- Work full-time or have a variable schedule
- Want to choose or bring your own instructor
- Prefer to learn at your own pace
- Want to rent the same aircraft for real cross-country trips while you train

Choose Part 141 if you:

- Can commit to a full-time, structured program
- Need an approved curriculum for benefits or career placement
- Want frequent stage checks and a cohort experience
